# How the Vegan Society Lost the Plot: The Drift from Cross's Definition

---

## The Definition That Started It All

In 1949, Leslie Cross — first Vice-President of the Vegan Society — wrote what remains the most philosophically precise articulation of what veganism actually is:

> *"The principle of the emancipation of animals from exploitation by man."*

And more specifically:

> *"The doctrine that man should live without exploiting animals."*

These two sentences are the foundation. They are clear, universal, and unambiguous. They place the concept of **exploitation** at the centre. They make a claim about **all humans** — not about personal preference or individual lifestyle. And they frame veganism as a **principle** and a **doctrine**: something with philosophical weight and moral force, not a dietary habit.

---

## The Current Definition

The Vegan Society's official definition today reads:

> *"Veganism is a philosophy and way of living which seeks to exclude — as far as is possible and practicable — all forms of exploitation of, and cruelty to, animals for food, clothing or any other purpose; and by extension, promotes the development and use of animal-free alternatives for the benefit of animals, humans and the environment."*

At first glance this appears consistent with Cross's formulation. It mentions exploitation. It mentions philosophy. It is broad in scope. But on closer examination, several specific departures from Cross's original have accumulated — and each one has had compounding consequences for how veganism is understood by the public.

---

## Where It Diverges

### 1. "Way of living" displaces the universal claim

Cross said *man* should live without exploiting animals — a universal moral claim about all humans. The current definition replaces this with *"a philosophy and way of living"* — which frames veganism as something an individual adopts, a personal orientation, a way one chooses to organise one's own life.

This is a significant shift. A universal moral claim ("exploitation is wrong, therefore no human should exploit animals") has a fundamentally different character from a personal lifestyle frame ("this is how I choose to live"). The former demands engagement with the world — if exploitation is universally wrong, you believe it is wrong when others do it too, which means advocacy is inherent to genuinely holding the principle. The latter implies you have simply chosen a particular way of living for yourself, with no claim on what others should do.

The phrase "way of living" is exactly the language used by critics and deflectors to reduce veganism to a personal choice — to something others are equally entitled to ignore. It is not neutral framing. It actively undermines the moral universality that Cross's definition made explicit.

### 2. "Cruelty" introduces a suffering-based frame that Cross specifically did not use

Cross's definition centres on **exploitation** — the use of animals as resources for human ends, regardless of how that use is carried out. The current definition adds *"cruelty"* alongside exploitation.

This matters because cruelty implies a question of method and intent: whether the exploitation is done with unnecessary roughness or pain. But exploitation without cruelty — a painlessly killed animal, a "humanely raised" farmed animal — is still exploitation. By adding cruelty as a parallel concern, the current definition opens the door to the interpretation that veganism is fundamentally about how animals are treated, not whether they are treated as resources at all.

This is the definitional basis for the welfarist misreading: if cruelty is the problem, then reducing cruelty is a solution. If exploitation is the problem, nothing short of ending it is.

### 3. The environmental and human benefit clause dilutes the moral centre

The current definition adds *"for the benefit of animals, humans and the environment"* — framing veganism partly as a response to environmental and human health concerns. While these are real and valid considerations, placing them inside the definition of veganism itself changes what veganism is. It becomes a multi-purpose position addressing several overlapping concerns, rather than a specific moral commitment about the wrongness of exploiting animals.

This conflation is visible in everyday discourse: "veganism is good for the planet," "veganism is healthier," "veganism reduces emissions." All may be true. None of them are what veganism is. Veganism is the position that animals should not be exploited. Environmental and health benefits are downstream effects, not the definition.

When the definition absorbs these concerns, it becomes easy to argue that you can achieve "what veganism is about" through other means — lab-grown meat, reduced consumption, carbon offsets — without ever engaging with the question of exploitation.

### 4. The active, advocacy dimension disappears

Cross's formulation — *"the doctrine that man should live without exploiting animals"* — implies universality, and universality implies advocacy. If you hold that all humans should live without exploiting animals, you necessarily believe exploitation is wrong when anyone does it. Remaining silent about ongoing exploitation while personally abstaining is an internally inconsistent position. The principle demands engagement.

The current definition contains no trace of this. It describes what veganism *seeks to exclude* and *promotes* in the abstract, but makes no claim that a person who holds this position has any responsibility beyond their own practice. This omission has produced a generation of "passive vegans" — people who do not consume animal products personally but treat that as the full expression of their commitment, with no sense that advocacy is either implied or necessary.

This passivity is not a cultural accident. It is the predictable consequence of a definition that frames veganism as a way of living rather than a universal moral commitment.

---

## The Consequences

These definitional shifts — individually modest, cumulatively significant — are not just academic. They have shaped how veganism is communicated, understood, and responded to at scale.

When people encounter veganism for the first time through the current definition's frame, they encounter it as: a lifestyle some people choose, focused on personal diet and consumption, motivated by a mix of animal welfare, environmental, and health concerns. They respond to that version. They evaluate it, find objections to it, adopt partial versions of it, and feel that by buying "humane" products or cutting back on meat they have engaged meaningfully with what veganism is about.

They have never encountered Cross's version: that the exploitation of animals is wrong, that this is a universal moral claim, that the appropriate response is to end participation in exploitation and to advocate for others to do the same.

The Vegan Society, as the originating institution of veganism, bears significant responsibility for this. The drift in its own definition — however gradual and however well-intentioned — has contributed directly to the public confusion it presumably exists to address.

---

## What Accuracy Requires

An accurate definition of veganism must do three things that the current definition does not:

**First**, it must make the universal moral claim explicit — not "a way some people live" but a doctrine about how humans should relate to other animals.

**Second**, it must centre exploitation rather than cruelty — the wrong is in the use, not only in the method.

**Third**, it must convey that genuine commitment to the principle is not passive — holding that exploitation is wrong means caring about exploitation wherever it occurs, which means advocacy is not optional.

Cross's 1949 formulation does all three in two sentences. The current definition does none of them clearly. That gap is not a minor semantic quibble. It is the difference between a moral principle with force and a lifestyle with branding.
