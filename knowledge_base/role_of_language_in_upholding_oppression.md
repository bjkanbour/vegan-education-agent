# The Role of Language in Upholding Oppression: Why Even Vegan Activists Must Reconsider Their Words

Language is not merely a tool for communication — it's a powerful lens through which we interpret the world. The words we choose can either reinforce oppressive systems or help dismantle them. Every system of domination, whether it be based on race, gender, or species, has relied on language to justify and maintain its power. In the context of animal exploitation, the language we use shapes our beliefs about whether animals are autonomous individuals or resources meant for human use.

Even among those who advocate for veganism and animal liberation, the terminology and phrases we use can inadvertently mirror the very mindset we seek to oppose. If our goal is to reject the notion that animals exist solely for our benefit, it is crucial that our language reflects that commitment. We must move away from words and slogans that imply compromise and instead embrace a discourse that clearly centres on ending exploitation.

---

## Food & Meat: How We Reinforce the Idea That Animals Are Edible

When we speak about food in terms of "alternatives" to animal products, the language often carries implicit assumptions:

- **Default Assumptions:** Phrases like "meat alternatives" or "plant-based chicken" imply that meat is the standard food item, while plant-based options are merely substitutes. This positions animal products as the norm against which all alternatives are measured.
- **Categorisation of Animals as Edible:** By constantly referring to animal-based products, even when discussing ethical or alternative options, we maintain the categorisation of animals as food. It suggests that the only way to improve the system is to make better versions of something that should not exist in the first place.
- **Normalisation of Consumption:** When language frames the conversation around finding "better alternatives," it inadvertently reinforces the idea that consuming animal products is acceptable, as long as it is done in a supposedly ethical or less harmful way.

**A stronger message:**
> "Animals were never food to begin with — only labelled that way by those who exploited them."
> "There is no such thing as 'ethical meat' because animals are not ours to consume."
> "Food is something we grow, not someone we take."

---

## Objectification in Vegan Language: "It" vs. "They"

Using "it" to refer to animals strips them of their individuality and reinforces the notion that they are objects or commodities rather than sentient beings.

- Using **"it"** implies that an animal is a thing rather than an individual with feelings, experiences, and a distinct identity.
- Persistent use of objectifying pronouns normalises viewing animals as mere objects or resources.
- It erases their individuality and reduces them to abstract entities devoid of personal identity.

**A stronger message:**
> "This bird was rescued from a factory farm. They are safe now."
> "Animals are not objects. They are beings with their own experiences and inherent worth."

---

## Language Framing Veganism as a Personal Journey, Lifestyle, and Identity

Terms such as "vegan journey," "vegan lifestyle," "vegan identity," and "way of living" are common in discussions about veganism. While these expressions can be relatable, they carry implications that divert attention from the political and ethical imperatives of vegan activism.

- **"Vegan Journey"** suggests veganism is a personal quest or evolving process rather than a unified political stance against oppression.
- **"Vegan Lifestyle / Way of Living"** frames veganism as a set of consumer habits among many, a trend or form of self-expression rather than a deliberate, ethical rejection of systems that exploit animals.
- **"Vegan Identity"** shifts focus to personal branding, distracting from the broader, collective struggle to dismantle the exploitative frameworks that underpin animal use.

When veganism is primarily described as a personal journey or lifestyle choice, the discourse centres on individual self-improvement. This obscures the need for systemic change.

**Alternative framing:** Instead of "vegan journey" or "lifestyle," use expressions that reflect commitment and resistance: "a commitment to ending exploitation" or "a stand against animal commodification."

---

## Principle vs. Practice: Bridging the Gap in Vegan Activism

There is often a notable disconnect between the radical principles that underpin the ethical stance and the everyday practices — both in language and action — that are employed. While the core belief is the complete rejection of animal exploitation, the language used in public discourse sometimes reflects compromises, making veganism appear as a consumer trend or lifestyle choice.

When there is a clear dissonance between core principles and public practices, the authenticity of the movement is questioned. A focus on incremental change can inadvertently legitimise systems of animal use. This incrementalism diverts attention from the more radical demand for complete abolition of exploitation.

**Aligning practice with principle:**
- Use language that reflects the uncompromising nature of the movement. Rather than framing veganism as a lifestyle choice, emphasise the inherent right of animals to live free from exploitation.
- Integrate radical ideals into personal narratives, explicitly connecting them to the broader political context.

## Utilitarianism, Veganism, and the Need for a Deontological Approach

Utilitarianism — most famously championed by Peter Singer — focuses on maximising overall well-being by reducing suffering. However, this consequentialist framework can sometimes lead to ethical compromises. Relying solely on utilitarian arguments may inadvertently justify certain uses of animals if they result in a net decrease in harm.

**The harms of a solely utilitarian framework:**
- Utilitarianism's focus on outcomes can sometimes lead to justifying practices that, while reducing overall suffering, still involve exploitation.
- Utilitarian rhetoric often centres on improving treatment rather than rejecting exploitation altogether. This focus can result in debates about what constitutes "humane" or "ethical" use, which implies there might be a morally acceptable way to use animals — a notion that undermines the abolitionist vision.
- The "crops death rebuttal" leverages utilitarian calculus. It deflects from the core injustice of exploiting sentient beings by comparing forms of harm rather than addressing whether animals should be treated as resources at all.

**A deontological approach:** Veganism asserts that animals possess inherent worth and should not be exploited under any circumstances. This perspective rejects any form of animal use, regardless of the net outcome in terms of reduced suffering. Exploitation is never acceptable. The focus shifts from weighing harms to upholding a fundamental ethical principle.

## The Problem with "Reducing Suffering" Language

The phrase "Veganism reduces suffering" is commonly used in advocacy. It appears compassionate and pragmatic. However, it shifts the focus away from the root issue: the belief that animals exist solely for human use.

**What "reduces suffering" implies:**
- Partial improvement is acceptable — the focus is on reducing harm rather than eliminating exploitation entirely.
- Incremental change over radical transformation is prioritised.
- Focus on treatment, not ownership — the language centres on how animals are treated within the system rather than challenging the fundamental idea that animals should be used at all.

**Stronger alternatives:**
> "Veganism is about rejecting the belief that animals are here for us to use."
> "The issue isn't how they are treated — the issue is that they are being used at all."

---

## Abuse vs. Exploitation: Understanding the Distinctions

In discussions about animal liberation, two terms often arise — **abuse** and **exploitation** — and they are sometimes used interchangeably. Each carries a distinct meaning:

**Abuse** involves direct, often violent actions that cause physical or psychological harm. It is typically tangible and highly visible. Focusing solely on abuse leads to debates about whether there is a "proper" or "humane" way to use animals, leaving intact the foundational exploitation.

**Exploitation** refers to the systemic processes and ideologies that reduce animals to their utility for human benefit. It is the framework that views animals as resources to be used, managed, and discarded. Not all forms of exploitation result in overt abuse — some practices might not immediately appear "abusive" but still contribute to the long-term devaluation of animal life.

**Why the distinction matters:**
- Many instances of abuse are symptomatic of a larger system that inherently devalues animal life.
- Focusing exclusively on abuse can lead to debates about acceptable treatment or conditions, inadvertently implying that there might be a proper way to use animals.
- Recognising exploitation as the core issue shifts the focus from treatment debates to questioning the entire notion of animal use.

**Stronger message:**
> "We must end not only the overt abuse of animals but also the systemic exploitation that makes such abuse acceptable."
> "True liberation requires dismantling the entire framework that views animals as resources, not merely improving their treatment."

---

## The Kindness Trap: "Be Kind, Be Vegan"

The slogan "Be kind, be vegan" suggests that veganism is a reflection of one's personal kindness or moral character. This framing:

1. **Individualises the issue** — makes veganism seem like a personal, optional decision rather than a necessary rejection of an unjust system.
2. **Dilutes the political message** — obscures the movement's deeper critique that the very idea of owning or using animals is inherently unjust.
3. **Reinforces the status quo** — suggests that simply being nicer to animals is enough, downplaying the need for structural change.

**Stronger alternatives:**
> "Veganism isn't about kindness — it's about rejecting the idea that animals exist for us."
> "Justice isn't an act of kindness; it's the recognition that others were never ours to control."

---

## Framing Veganism as a Consumer Choice

Phrases such as "Buy cruelty-free!" or "Support plant-based businesses!" reduce veganism to a matter of market choices. This framing shifts the focus from the fundamental rejection of animal exploitation to improving consumer behaviour within a system that still treats animals as commodities.

- It suggests that ethical transformation is primarily a market-driven process.
- It frames veganism as a lifestyle tweak rather than a call to reject an exploitative framework.
- It allows industries to reform their products without addressing the deeper issues of exploitation and ownership.

**Stronger message:**
> "Animals don't need more vegan products in stores. They need a world where they aren't seen as products at all."
> "Justice is not a market trend — it's the recognition that animals were never ours to use in the first place."

---

## The Danger of "Personal Choice" Language

Phrases like "I choose to be vegan" and "you should consider going vegan" position veganism as a lifestyle option available to anyone, reducing it to a matter of personal taste or preference.

- The language minimises systemic injustice by implying the burden of change rests solely on the individual.
- When veganism is framed purely as a personal choice, it detracts from the underlying message that animal exploitation is a systemic problem.
- It can lead to debates over lifestyle preferences rather than engaging with the ethics of exploitation.

**Stronger message:**
> "Veganism is not a personal choice — it's the rejection of exploitation."
> "Justice is not something to consider — it's the recognition that animals were never ours to use."

---

## Final Thought: Are We Speaking in Alignment with Liberation?

Every conversation, every article, every social media post is an opportunity to challenge the status quo. Our words can be the spark that ignites change, or they can inadvertently fuel the very systems we aim to overthrow.

> "Animals do not need us to grant them rights, they need us to stop exploiting them."
> "Our language must reject the notion that any form of exploitation is acceptable."

As we continue toward animal liberation, commit to a language that unequivocally rejects exploitation. Speak in terms that celebrate the individuality, dignity, and intrinsic worth of every living being. In doing so, we not only honour the animals we seek to protect, but we also pave the way for a society rooted in true justice.
