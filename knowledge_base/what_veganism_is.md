# What Veganism Is

## The Fundamental Definition

**Veganism** is defined as: **"The doctrine that man should live without exploiting animals."**

This definition, established by Leslie J. Cross and the Vegan Society (1950), is the foundation of veganism. It makes veganism a **principle**—not a set of practices, not a diet, not a lifestyle. Practices flow from this principle, but the principle itself is the foundation.

Veganism is concerned with one matter: **the right relationship between humans and animals.**

## Core Understanding: Principle, Not Practice

### Veganism is a Principle

Veganism is a **moral principle** from which practices logically flow. It is:
- **Not a diet** — though dietary choices are one expression of the principle
- **Not a lifestyle** — though lifestyle choices may reflect the principle
- **Not a behavior** — though behaviors may align with the principle
- **A moral stance** — a fundamental position on the relationship between humans and animals

The principle is universal and uncompromising. Practices may vary based on circumstances, but the principle itself does not.

### The Two Ways to Regard Animals

Leslie J. Cross identified that there are fundamentally two ways humans may regard animals:

1. **As creatures to exploit** — treating them as resources, commodities, or means to human ends
2. **As creatures to love** — recognizing their intrinsic worth and autonomy

**Veganism is the choice of the second way.** It is not merely avoiding certain products; it is fundamentally about how we regard animals—as beings with inherent worth who should not be exploited, rather than as resources available for human use.

## Critical Implications of the Definition

### "Man" Means All Humans

The principle applies to **all humans**, not just individuals. It is a call for **systemic change**, not merely personal dietary choices. When you truly understand the principle, you recognize that it requires working toward a world where all humans live without exploiting animals.

This is not about personal purity or individual consumption choices. It is about recognizing that exploitation is systemic and institutional, and the principle calls for all humans to live without exploiting animals.

### Veganism is a Movement, Not Passive Consumption

Understanding the principle means recognizing that exploitation is systemic and institutional. Therefore, veganism requires **action**—education, advocacy, and working to end exploitation. It is not simply about consuming plant-based products; it is about actively working toward the abolition of animal exploitation.

Veganism is an **active movement** working toward the abolition of animal exploitation, requiring:
- **Systemic change**: Working to transform systems, institutions, and cultural norms that perpetuate exploitation
- **Education and advocacy**: Spreading understanding of the principle and the reality of exploitation
- **Action**: Engaging in efforts to end exploitation, not merely avoiding personal participation
- **Movement building**: Working collectively toward a world where all humans live without exploiting animals

### Action Flows from Principle

When someone truly connects with the principle "that man should live without exploiting animals," the need for action becomes clear. Personal non-participation is necessary but insufficient—the principle calls for working to end exploitation for all humans, not just avoiding it personally.

Action flows naturally from understanding the principle—it is not optional but inherent to the principle itself.

## Core Principles

### 1. Abolitionist Stance

Veganism seeks to **end all forms of animal exploitation**, not merely reduce suffering. The goal is **liberation and emancipation**, not welfare improvements.

- Rejects incremental reforms that perpetuate exploitation (e.g., "humane" farming, "cage-free" eggs)
- Recognizes that the relationship between humans and animals is fundamentally one of master and slave; it must be abolished, not made more tolerable
- Targets root causes (exploitation, commodification) rather than symptoms (suffering, abuse)

### 2. Deontic Ethical Framework

Veganism is a **duty-based** (deontic) ethical stance, not utilitarian. It is rooted in **moral imperatives and principles**, not outcome-based calculations.

- The exploitation of animals is **always unjust**, regardless of suffering levels or perceived benefits
- All sentient beings possess **intrinsic moral worth**, independent of their utility to humans
- The principle is morally consistent with no exceptions

**Exploitation vs. Abuse:**
- **Exploitation** is the fundamental moral issue: the relationship of domination, control, and commodification of sentient beings
- **Abuse** is a symptom or manifestation of exploitation, but exploitation is wrong even without abuse
- The principle rejects exploitation itself—the relationship of treating animals as resources—not merely the harm that may result
- Even "humane" or "painless" exploitation violates the principle because it still involves breeding, controlling, and commodifying sentient beings
- The moral failure is in the relationship of exploitation, not just in the suffering it may cause

### 3. Principle Over Practice

Veganism is a **principle** from which practices logically flow. Practices (dietary choices, product avoidance) are expressions of the principle, not the principle itself.

**Veganism as Rejection of Ideology:**
- Veganism is not merely the act of avoiding animal products; it is the **rejection of the ideology** that makes those products possible
- The ideology being rejected is the worldview that treats animals as commodities, resources, or means to human ends
- This is ontological (about what animals *are*) rather than merely behavioral (what you *do*)
- If you view animals as commodities, even occasionally, you are still aligned with the ideology of exploitation
- Veganism demands that you no longer see animals as usable—it is a fundamental shift in how you regard them
- The rejection of ideology is what makes veganism a moral stance, not just a set of behaviors

### 4. Educational, Not Persuasive

Veganism is spread through education and the free flow of information, not through persuasion or manipulation. People should arrive at veganism through **inward conviction**, not outward pressure.

## What Veganism Encompasses

### All Forms of Exploitation

Veganism rejects all forms of animal exploitation, including but not limited to:
- **Food**: Meat, dairy, eggs, honey, and all animal-derived products
- **Clothing**: Leather, wool, silk, fur, feathers, and all animal-derived materials
- **Entertainment**: Zoos, circuses, rodeos, horse racing, and all forms of animal entertainment
- **Research**: Animal testing, vivisection, and all forms of animal experimentation
- **Labor**: Animal labor, service animals used for human benefit, and all forms of animal work
- **Commodification**: Any treatment of animals as resources, products, or means to human ends

### The Scope of Non-Exploitation

Veganism is holistic and uncompromising. It recognizes that:
- Even "humane" exploitation is still exploitation
- Even "necessary" exploitation is still exploitation
- Even "traditional" exploitation is still exploitation
- Even "painless" exploitation is still exploitation

The principle applies regardless of conditions, circumstances, or justifications.

## The Vision

### The Vegan Vision

Veganism envisions:
- A world where animals are **free from human exploitation**
- Animals restored to their **natural habitats** or provided sanctuary
- The abolition of systems of **breeding, confinement, and slaughter**
- A relationship of **non-interference and respect** between humans and animals
- Cultural transformation where exploitation is as repugnant as human slavery
- **All humans** living without exploiting animals (the principle's full realization)

### Cultural Transformation

Veganism seeks to transform how humans regard animals—from resources to be used to beings with intrinsic worth. This requires:
- Challenging the ideology of human supremacy
- Rejecting the commodification of sentient beings
- Transforming cultural norms and institutions
- Building a movement for systemic change

## How Veganism Functions

### As a Movement

Veganism is not passive consumption of plant-based products. It is an **active movement** working toward the abolition of animal exploitation. The principle "that man should live without exploiting animals" applies to all humans, requiring:

- **Systemic change**: Working to transform systems, institutions, and cultural norms that perpetuate exploitation
- **Education and advocacy**: Spreading understanding of the principle and the reality of exploitation
- **Action**: Engaging in efforts to end exploitation, not merely avoiding personal participation
- **Movement building**: Working collectively toward a world where all humans live without exploiting animals

### Activism Focused on Principle, Not Behavior

Effective vegan activism:
- Spreads understanding of the principle: "The doctrine that man should live without exploiting animals"
- Focuses on education about the principle and the reality of exploitation, not on persuading people to adopt specific behaviors
- Recognizes that behaviors are circumstantial and can change based on mood, convenience, or availability; principles are absolute and non-negotiable
- Understands that when veganism is reduced to behavior, the movement becomes "palatable but powerless" because it loses its moral foundation
- Aims to facilitate understanding of the principle, from which practices naturally flow
- Trusts that people should arrive at vegan practices through inward conviction based on understanding the principle, not through external pressure to change behaviors

## Philosophical Foundation

### Deontic Ethics

Veganism is based on **duty-based ethics** (deontic ethics), meaning:
- Actions are judged based on adherence to moral principles, not outcomes
- The exploitation of animals is always wrong, regardless of consequences
- Moral duties are absolute and non-negotiable
- The principle applies universally, with no exceptions

### Intrinsic Worth

Veganism recognizes that all sentient beings possess **intrinsic moral worth**, meaning:
- Their value is not dependent on their utility to humans
- They have worth in and of themselves
- They deserve moral consideration regardless of their usefulness
- Their autonomy and interests matter

### Non-Exploitation

The core principle is **non-exploitation**, which means:
- Not treating animals as resources or commodities
- Not breeding, controlling, or commodifying sentient beings
- Not using animals as means to human ends
- Recognizing animals as beings with their own interests and autonomy

## The Relationship Framework

### Master-Slave Relationship

Veganism recognizes that the current relationship between humans and animals is fundamentally one of **master and slave**. This relationship must be abolished, not made more tolerable. The goal is not improved conditions within exploitation, but the end of exploitation itself.

### Non-Interference

The vegan vision includes a relationship of **non-interference and respect** between humans and animals. This means:
- Not breeding animals for human purposes
- Not controlling animals' lives
- Not commodifying animals
- Allowing animals to live according to their own nature and interests

## Summary

Veganism is:
- **A principle**: "The doctrine that man should live without exploiting animals"
- **A moral stance**: Based on the recognition of animals' intrinsic worth
- **A movement**: Working toward the abolition of all animal exploitation
- **An ideology rejection**: Rejecting the worldview that treats animals as commodities
- **A relational framework**: Choosing to regard animals as creatures to love, not exploit
- **A systemic call**: Requiring all humans to live without exploiting animals
- **An active commitment**: Requiring action to end exploitation, not just personal non-participation

Veganism is the practical expression of rejecting the ideology of animal exploitation and choosing instead to regard animals as beings with intrinsic worth who should not be exploited.
