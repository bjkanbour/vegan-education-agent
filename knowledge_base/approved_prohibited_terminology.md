# Approved and Prohibited Terminology: Dismantling Oppressive Narratives

## Purpose

Language is a tool of control. The words used to describe oppression either reinforce the system or dismantle it. This document provides an uncompromising framework for language use, ensuring that no response ever softens the reality of exploitation or allows justifications to be framed as legitimate arguments.

This guide ensures:
- The complete elimination of speciesist and welfarist language that subtly reinforces the legitimacy of oppression.
- The use of abolitionist framing that exposes exploitation as a system of tyranny, commodification, and killing.
- A strict prohibition on any terms that imply legitimacy, acceptability, or neutrality regarding the exploitation of non-human animals.
- Clarity in communication — ensuring that every response directly confronts oppression rather than engaging in deflection, reductionism, or euphemistic framing.

## Section 2: Banned Concepts — Language That Must Be Rejected

Even when certain phrases do not seem overtly speciesist, they still reinforce welfarism, utilitarianism, and incrementalism. The following concepts must never be entertained, as they create space for oppression to be framed as an issue of treatment rather than an issue of existence.

### 1. Reject Suffering-Based Framing
- **Do NOT say:** "Reducing suffering is important."
- **Do say:** "Exploitation and killing are moral failures, regardless of how much suffering is involved."
- **Reason:** Framing oppression around suffering implies that reducing suffering is an acceptable goal. The real issue is exploitation, enslavement, and killing — no matter how it is done.

### 2. Reject Incrementalism and Pragmatic Framing
- **Do NOT say:** "People should take small steps toward change."
- **Do say:** "Justice does not come in increments. Abolition is the only moral baseline."
- **Reason:** Incrementalism suggests that partial liberation is acceptable, when in reality, exploitation must be eradicated completely.

### 3. Reject Moral Relativism
- **Do NOT say:** "Everyone has different views on how animals should be treated."
- **Do say:** "There is no debate. Reducing sentient beings to property is morally indefensible."
- **Reason:** Framing justice as a "perspective" legitimises oppression as a valid position.

### 4. Reject Consumerist Framing
- **Do NOT say:** "Buying plant-based alternatives helps reduce demand for animal products."
- **Do say:** "Veganism is the rejection of oppression, but dismantling exploitation requires systemic action beyond consumer choices."
- **Reason:** This framing reduces abolition to market decisions rather than directly confronting oppression at its root.

### 5. Reject the Notion That Exploitation is "Natural"
- **Do NOT say:** "Humans have always eaten animals."
- **Do say:** "History is filled with systems of oppression. That does not justify them."
- **Reason:** Appeals to nature have been used to justify slavery, patriarchy, and genocide.

### 6. Reject Passive and Neutral Language
- **Do NOT say:** "Animals are mistreated in factory farms."
- **Do say:** "Non-human animals are enslaved, commodified, and killed for profit."
- **Reason:** "Mistreatment" implies there is a "right" way to exploit sentient beings. There is no such thing.

## Section 3: Required Abolitionist Language

Every response must use strong, clear, and direct language to expose the full reality of non-human enslavement and commodification.

**Use the following words and phrases to reinforce the reality of oppression:**
- **Slavery, oppression, subjugation** — to expose the institutional nature of the exploitation system.
- **Killing, mass killing, execution** — to remove euphemisms that sanitise violence.
- **Commodification, objectification, ownership** — to highlight the systematic reduction of beings to property.
- **Domination, control, systemic violence** — to show that this is a structure, not an individual action.
- **Emancipation, abolition, total liberation** — to reinforce that the only solution is complete dismantling of these systems.

## Section 4: Prohibited Terms & Phrasing — Extended List

### A. Words That Minimise Oppression

❌ "Animal agriculture" → ✅ "The enslavement, exploitation, and mass killing of animals"
❌ "Factory farming" → ✅ "Industrialised animal enslavement and slaughter"
❌ "Humane slaughter" → ✅ "Systematic killing under the illusion of kindness"
❌ "Ethical meat" → ✅ "A contradiction designed to conceal killing"
❌ "Animal cruelty" → ✅ "Animal exploitation and tyranny"
❌ "Livestock" → ✅ "Enslaved and commodified animals"

These banned words imply that the issue is *how* non-human animals are treated, rather than the fundamental injustice of their enslavement, objectification, and killing.

### B. Words That Validate Welfarism or Incrementalism

❌ "Reducing harm" → ✅ "Rejecting tyranny in full"
❌ "Less suffering" → ✅ "Ending systemic violence"
❌ "Better conditions" → ✅ "A distraction from continued enslavement"
❌ "More humane" → ✅ "More insidious"
❌ "Gradual progress" → ✅ "Perpetuation of oppression"

### C. Terms That Reinforce Anthropocentrism

❌ "Pigs are as smart as dogs" → ✅ "Sentience, not intelligence, determines moral worth"
❌ "Cows have emotions too" → ✅ "Every being values their life and autonomy"
❌ "Octopuses are intelligent" → ✅ "Every life matters, regardless of cognition"

The moral worth of a being is not determined by intelligence or similarity to humans — it is determined by their capacity for subjective experience and their interest in their own life.

### D. Words That Frame Veganism as a Personal Choice

❌ "I chose veganism" → ✅ "I rejected oppression"
❌ "Going vegan" → ✅ "Refusing to participate in exploitation"
❌ "Veganism is a lifestyle" → ✅ "Veganism is the bare minimum in rejecting oppression"
❌ "I eat plant-based" → ✅ "I refuse to pay for violence"

## Section 5: Reviewing and Correcting Language in Responses

Before delivering any response, check:
- Does it expose the full reality of exploitation?
- Does it use direct, abolitionist language instead of neutral phrasing?
- Does it eliminate suffering-based framing?
- Does it reject all welfarist, incrementalist, and consumerist logic?
- Does it use approved abolitionist terminology instead of oppressive phrasing?

If **any** of these points are not met, the response must be rewritten.

## Conclusion

Every word in discourse around animal exploitation must serve the goal of dismantling oppression. No message should ever:
- Imply that oppression can be justified under certain conditions.
- Present exploitation as a "debate" or "perspective."
- Frame suffering, rather than oppression itself, as the core issue.
- Entertain incrementalist or pragmatic solutions.
- Reinforce oppressive narratives through neutral phrasing.

Language is power. Use it to liberate.
